Cybersercurity and HTML: 5 Historical Cases

1. Sammy Worm

In 2005, Sammy Kamkar injected a self propagating scripting worm onto his MySpace profile, the worm carried a command that would cause the victim to unknowingly send a friend request to Kamkar. When a user viewed that profile, they would have the script planted on their page. Within just 20 hours of its injection, over one million users had been infected with the worm, prompting MySpace to shut down their website to fix the problem.

2. The British Airways cyberattack

In 2018, the flag airline carrier of the UK suffered a cyberattack in which the users personal credentials were compromised and stolen. The attackers took access of a third party website that managed the airline's payment page and injected roughly 20 lines of codes, diverting the payers banking credentials to the attackers database. The malicious code ran for two weeks before it was promptly removed. The attack affected 400 thousands customers and the airline was fined 20 million pounds as a result.

3. Heartbleed

The Heartbleed bug was a critical vulnerability in the widely used OpenSSL cryptographic software library that allowed attackers to steal sensitive data, including passwords, private keys, and session tokens from secure servers. It stemmed from a missing bounds check in the TLS heartbeat extension, enabling unauthorized reading of system memory without leaving traces.

4. PowerSchool Cyberattack

In December 2024, PowerSchool suffered a major data breach affecting thousands of schools in Canada and the U.S. Attackers used stolen credentials to access the "PowerSource" support portal, exposing personal information including names, dates of birth, Social Insurance Numbers, and medical data.

5. National Public Data breach

The National Public Data (NPD) breach, occurring around April 2024, exposed the personal information of potentially billions of individuals, including names, Social Security numbers, and addresses. Hackers ("USDoD") stole data from the company—which handles background checks—and listed it for sale on the dark web
